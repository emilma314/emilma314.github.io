The Schrödinger Equation

In quantum mechanics, particles don't have definite positions. Instead they have a wavefunction that describes where they are likely to be found once measured. The Schrödinger equation describes how that wavefunction evolves over time.

$$i\hbar\,\frac{\partial \psi}{\partial t} = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\nabla^2\psi + V\psi$$

Wavefunctions

The wavefunction $\psi$ (psi), is a mathematical function that assigns a (complex) number to every point in space.

$|\psi(x)|^2$ is the probability of finding the particle at position $x$.
$|\psi|^2$ is bounded between 0 (no chance of observing the particle) and 1 (complete certainty of observing the particle) at each location.

The Schrödinger equation describes how $\psi$ changes over time, analogously to how Newton's second law describes how position changes over time in classical mechanics.

Dissecting the Equation

Written out, the time-dependent Schrödinger equation is:

$$i\hbar\,\frac{\partial \psi}{\partial t} = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\nabla^2\psi + V\psi$$

The left side tracks how $\psi$ changes in time. The right side computes the total energy of the particle — kinetic energy (the $\nabla^2$ term) plus potential energy ($V$). Thus, the equation says that the time evolution of the wavefunction is driven by the particle's total energy.

Symbol Name What it means
$\psi$ psi (wavefunction) A complex-valued function of space and time. Its square $|\psi|^2$ gives the probability of finding the particle at a given location.
$i$ imaginary unit $\sqrt{-1}$. Its presence is why $\psi$ must be complex, and why the solutions oscillate rather than decay.
$\hbar$ h-bar Planck's constant divided by $2\pi$. An extremely small number ($\approx 10^{-34}$ J·s) that sets the scale of quantum effects.
$m$ mass The mass of the particle.
$\nabla^2\psi$ Laplacian of psi Measures how curved $\psi$ is in space, a higher dimensional generalization of the second derivative. Highly curved wavefunctions correspond to high kinetic energy, meaning that a more "wiggly" wavefunction generally means faster motion.
$V$ potential energy The environment the particle is in. A box, a spring, or an atom's electric field are all encoded in $V(x)$.

Schrödinger's original argument was partly heuristic, more of an educated guess guided by wave mechanics and the de Broglie relations. Here's the logic:

1

Start from classical energy conservation. For a particle with momentum $p$ and potential $V$:

$$E = \frac{p^2}{2m} + V$$
2

De Broglie (1924) proposed that particles have wave-like properties. For a plane wave $\psi = e^{i(kx - \omega t)}$ (looks familiar? remember the solution to the waves equation?), differentiating gives:

$$\frac{\partial \psi}{\partial t} = -i\omega\psi \implies E = \hbar\omega \implies E\psi = i\hbar\frac{\partial\psi}{\partial t}$$
$$\frac{\partial^2\psi}{\partial x^2} = -k^2\psi \implies p = \hbar k \implies p^2\psi = -\hbar^2\frac{\partial^2\psi}{\partial x^2}$$
3

Substitute these operator replacements into the energy equation $E = p^2/2m + V$:

$$i\hbar\frac{\partial\psi}{\partial t} = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{\partial^2\psi}{\partial x^2} + V\psi$$

In 3D, $\partial^2/\partial x^2$ becomes $\nabla^2$, giving the full equation. This is ultimately a postulate — we trust it because its predictions match experiments with extraordinary precision.

Energy levels in a box

One of the most striking results of the Schrödinger equation is quantization: a particle trapped in a box can only have certain specific energies. It can't have any energy it wants — only the allowed levels.

Each allowed energy comes with a specific wavefunction shape. Higher energy means more wiggles. The number of wiggles is called the quantum number $n$.

A particle trapped between two walls. Each row is an allowed energy state.
The curve shows the wavefunction $\psi$ — squaring it gives the probability of finding the particle there.
There is no state between these levels. The particle must jump from one to another.

Connection to the continuity equation

Remember the continuity equation from the first page? It turns out the Schrödinger equation automatically satisfies it.

If you define the probability density $\rho = |\psi|^2$ and work through the math, you can show that $\rho$ satisfies:

$$\frac{\partial |\psi|^2}{\partial t} + \nabla \cdot \mathbf{J} = 0$$
This means probability is conserved. The total probability of finding the particle somewhere stays exactly 1 for all time — the particle doesn't vanish. This isn't assumed; it falls directly out of the Schrödinger equation.

What does this equation explain?

The Schrödinger equation is the foundation of all of modern chemistry and much of physics. Here are three things it directly predicts:

Chemistry

Atomic orbitals

The shapes of electron clouds in atoms — s, p, d orbitals — are solutions to the Schrödinger equation for the hydrogen potential.

Semiconductors

Transistors & LEDs

The band structure of silicon and the behavior of electrons in semiconductors all follow from applying this equation to crystal lattices.

Quantum computing

Qubits

A qubit is just a quantum system with two energy levels. Its evolution is governed directly by the Schrödinger equation.

Key takeaways

  • Particles in quantum mechanics don't have definite positions — they have wavefunctions.
  • $|\psi|^2$ gives the probability of finding the particle at a given point.
  • The Schrödinger equation tells you how $\psi$ evolves over time.
  • A trapped particle can only have certain discrete energies — it's quantized.
  • Higher energy states have more "wiggles" in their wavefunction.
  • Probability is automatically conserved — the particle never disappears.
