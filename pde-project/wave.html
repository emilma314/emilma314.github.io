The Wave Equation

When you disturb something locally, the disturbance spreads outward through space. That's what a wave is.

$$\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial t^2} = v^2\nabla^2 u$$

The Equation

The wave equation describes how disturbances propagate. A displacement at one point affects nearby points, which then affect points farther away, creating a traveling wave.

Curvature creates acceleration in the opposite direction. If part of a wave is bent upward, it accelerates downward. If it's bent downward, it accelerates upward.

The equation connects two ideas:

$$\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial t^2} = v^2\nabla^2 u$$

The left side measures how the displacement accelerates in time. The right side measures how curved the wave is in space.

The Symbols

Symbol Name What it means
$u(x,t)$ wave displacement The quantity that is moving. For a string, this is the vertical displacement. For sound it can represent pressure variation, and for light it can represent an electric field.
$x$ position Your location in space.
$t$ time Tracks how the wave changes.
$\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial t^2}$ second time derivative The acceleration of the wave at a point.
$\nabla^2u$ Laplacian Measures spatial curvature, like a second derivative. Peaks have negative curvature, whereas valleys have positive curvature.
$v$ wave speed The speed at which disturbances move through the medium.

Solutions

The wave equation does not have a single solution. Different initial conditions create different waves.

One important solution is the traveling sine wave:

$$u(x,t)=A\sin(kx-\omega t)$$

This wave moves to the right without changing shape. The amplitude $A$ determines its height, $k$ determines its wavelength, and $\omega$ determines how rapidly it oscillates.

The wave equation produces moving patterns. Disturbances travel through space instead of instantly affecting everything.

Even very complicated waves can be built from many sine waves added together. This idea is the foundation of Fourier analysis.

In fact, the most general one-dimensional solution is:

$$u(x,t)=f(x-vt)+g(x+vt)$$

This says every solution can be viewed as the sum of a right-moving wave and a left-moving wave, each traveling at speed $v$.

Why waves move

In a stretched string, if one section is curved upward, tension pulls it back down. That restoring force creates acceleration, causing the disturbance to propagate through the string.

The wave equation says that spatial curvature determines how the wave accelerates in time.

Where does this appear?

The wave equation appears everywhere in physics. Different systems produce different kinds of waves, but the mathematics is nearly identical.

Vibrating strings: $$u(x,t)=\text{vertical displacement}$$
Sound waves: $$u(x,t)=\text{air pressure variation}$$
Electromagnetic waves: $$u(x,t)=\text{electric or magnetic field}$$

Key takeaways

  • The wave equation describes propagating disturbances.
  • Spatial curvature creates acceleration.
  • The equation is second order in time, so both position and velocity matter.
  • Waves transport energy and information through space.
  • The same PDE governs strings, sound, light, and many quantum systems.
