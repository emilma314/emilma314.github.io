The Continuity Equation

Stuff doesn't just appear or disappear. If density decreases somewhere, something must be flowing away.

$$\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} + \nabla \cdot \mathbf{J} =0$$

What does it say?

The continuity equation is a conservation law. It says that whatever you're tracking — mass, charge, probability, people — cannot spontaneously appear or vanish. It can only move from place to place.

If more stuff flows out of a tiny region than flows in, the amount inside must decrease.

The equation connects two ideas:

$$\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} = -\nabla\cdot\mathbf{J}$$

The left side measures how density changes over time. The right side measures how much flow spreads outward.

A local conservation law

Imagine a tiny region of space. If more stuff leaves the region than enters, the density inside must decrease.

Positive divergence means flow spreads outward, so the density inside decreases.

Where does this appear?

The exact same PDE appears across physics. Only the interpretation changes.

Fluid mechanics: $$\rho = \text{mass density}$$
Electromagnetism: $$\rho = \text{charge density}$$
Quantum mechanics: $$\rho = |\psi|^2$$

Key takeaways

  • The continuity equation expresses conservation.
  • Density changes only because stuff flows.
  • Positive divergence means stuff spreads outward.
  • Negative divergence means stuff converges inward.
  • The same PDE appears throughout physics.
