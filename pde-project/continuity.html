The Continuity Equation

Stuff doesn't just appear or disappear. If density decreases somewhere, something must be flowing away.

$$\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} + \nabla \cdot \mathbf{J} = 0$$

What does it say?

The continuity equation is a conservation law. It says that whatever you're tracking — mass, charge, probability, people — cannot spontaneously appear or vanish. It can only move from place to place.

If more stuff flows out of a tiny region than flows in, the amount inside must decrease — by exactly that much.

Rearranged, the equation says:

$$\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} = -\nabla \cdot \mathbf{J}$$

The left side is how fast the density is changing at a point. The right side is how much the flow is spreading outward from that point. They are always equal and opposite.

Breaking down the symbols

Symbol Name What it means
$\rho$ rho — density How much "stuff" is packed into a tiny region at a given point in space. In a fluid it's mass per volume. In electromagnetism it's charge per volume. In quantum mechanics it's probability per volume.
$\mathbf{J}$ J — flux A vector that describes the flow of stuff. It has both a magnitude (how much is flowing) and a direction (which way). Think of it as an arrow attached to every point in space, showing the current.
$\partial \rho / \partial t$ time derivative of ρ How fast the density is changing right now at this point. Negative means density is dropping; positive means it's growing.
$\nabla \cdot \mathbf{J}$ divergence of J How much the flow is "spreading out" from a point — see below.

What is divergence?

Divergence is the trickiest symbol in the equation, so it's worth pausing on. $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{J}$ at a point measures whether the flow arrows near that point are spreading outward or converging inward.

Imagine standing in the middle of a room watching people walk around you:

Positive divergence — everyone near you is walking away. Like standing at the center of an explosion. The flow is spreading out, so whatever is here is leaving. Density here will decrease.

Negative divergence — everyone is walking toward you. Like a drain pulling things in. Stuff is converging, so density here will increase.

Zero divergence — as much flows in as flows out. Density stays the same.

The continuity equation says these two things are always linked: positive divergence forces density down; negative divergence forces it up. They always balance exactly.

The formal proof uses only one key theorem from calculus — the divergence theorem.

1

Pick any fixed volume $V$ with boundary surface $S$. The total amount of stuff inside is:

$$Q(t) = \int_V \rho\; dV$$
2

Conservation means $Q$ can only change by stuff flowing across $S$. Outward flux decreases $Q$, so:

$$\frac{dQ}{dt} = -\oint_S \mathbf{J} \cdot d\mathbf{A}$$

The left side is also $\int_V \frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t}\,dV$ since $V$ is fixed.

3

The divergence theorem converts the surface integral to a volume integral:

$$\oint_S \mathbf{J} \cdot d\mathbf{A} = \int_V \nabla \cdot \mathbf{J}\;dV$$
4

Combining everything:

$$\int_V \!\left(\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} + \nabla \cdot \mathbf{J}\right) dV = 0$$

This must hold for every possible volume $V$. The only way that works is if the integrand itself is zero everywhere:

$$\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} + \nabla \cdot \mathbf{J} = 0$$

See it in action

Water flows through a pipe with a narrow section in the middle. Because water can't appear or disappear, squeezing into a narrower pipe forces the particles to crowd together and speed up. When the pipe widens again, they spread out and slow down.

This is the continuity equation made visible: the density and the flow speed always adjust together so that nothing is created or lost.

Particles crowd together and speed up in the narrow section, then spread out and slow down on the other side. The total number of particles is conserved at all times.

Where does this appear?

The exact same PDE appears across physics with different interpretations of $\rho$ and $\mathbf{J}$.

Fluid mechanics:   $\rho = $ mass density,   $\mathbf{J} = \rho\mathbf{v}$
Electromagnetism:   $\rho = $ charge density,   $\mathbf{J} = $ current density
Quantum mechanics:   $\rho = |\psi|^2$,   $\mathbf{J} = \frac{\hbar}{2mi}(\psi^*\nabla\psi - \psi\nabla\psi^*)$

In quantum mechanics this is especially striking: the Schrödinger equation (which we cover on the next page) automatically satisfies the continuity equation. That's how we know the total probability of finding a particle always stays at 1 — it falls out of the math, not as an assumption.

Key takeaways

  • The continuity equation expresses conservation — nothing appears or disappears.
  • $\rho$ is density; $\mathbf{J}$ is the flux vector showing how stuff flows.
  • Positive divergence means flow is spreading outward → density decreases.
  • Negative divergence means flow is converging inward → density increases.
  • The same PDE governs mass, charge, and probability.
