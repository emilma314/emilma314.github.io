The Continuity Equation

Stuff doesn't just appear or disappear. If density is decreasing somewhere, something must be flowing out. This single idea, made precise, gives us the continuity equation.

$$\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} + \nabla \cdot \mathbf{J} = 0$$

What does it say?

The continuity equation is a conservation law. It says that whatever you're tracking — mass, charge, probability, people — can't spontaneously appear or vanish. It can only move from place to place.

In plain English:

If the density at some point is decreasing, it's because stuff is flowing away from that point. If density is increasing, stuff is flowing in. The two are always exactly balanced.

That's the whole equation. The math just makes "stuff flowing away" precise using a concept called divergence.

Breaking down the symbols

Here's what each piece means:

Symbol Name What it means
$\rho$ rho (density) How much "stuff" is packed into a tiny region at a given point. Could be mass per volume, charge per volume, number of people per square meter, etc.
$\mathbf{J}$ J (flux) A vector describing how much stuff is flowing, and in which direction. Think of it as an arrow at every point in space showing the flow.
$\partial \rho / \partial t$ time derivative of ρ How fast the density is changing at this point right now. Negative means density is dropping; positive means it's growing.
$\nabla \cdot \mathbf{J}$ divergence of J How much the flow is "spreading out" from this point. Positive divergence = flow pointing away in all directions (like a fountain). Negative divergence = flow converging inward (like a drain).

So the equation $\partial\rho/\partial t + \nabla\cdot\mathbf{J} = 0$ literally reads: "the rate of change of density plus the outward flow equals zero" — meaning any decrease in density is exactly accounted for by outward flow. Nothing extra, nothing missing.

This is the formal proof. You need to know about volume integrals and the divergence theorem.

1

Pick any fixed volume $V$ in space with boundary surface $S$. The total amount of stuff inside is:

$$Q(t) = \int_V \rho(\mathbf{r},t)\;dV$$
2

Conservation means $Q$ can only change by stuff flowing across $S$. Outward flux decreases $Q$, so:

$$\frac{dQ}{dt} = -\oint_S \mathbf{J}\cdot d\mathbf{A}$$

The left side can also be written $\int_V \frac{\partial\rho}{\partial t}\,dV$ since $V$ is fixed.

3

Use the divergence theorem to convert the surface integral into a volume integral:

$$\oint_S \mathbf{J}\cdot d\mathbf{A} = \int_V \nabla\cdot\mathbf{J}\;dV$$
4

Combining steps 2 and 3:

$$\int_V \left(\frac{\partial\rho}{\partial t} + \nabla\cdot\mathbf{J}\right)dV = 0$$

This must hold for every possible volume $V$. The only continuous function that integrates to zero over every domain is zero itself. So the integrand must be zero everywhere:

$$\boxed{\frac{\partial\rho}{\partial t} + \nabla\cdot\mathbf{J} = 0}$$

Where does this equation show up?

The exact same equation — same symbols, same structure — appears across completely different fields of physics. Only the interpretation of $\rho$ and $\mathbf{J}$ changes.

Fluid Mechanics

Mass conservation

Water in a pipe can't pile up or vanish. What flows in must flow out.

$\rho$ =mass density
$\mathbf{J}$ =$\rho\mathbf{v}$
conservesmass
Electromagnetism

Charge conservation

Electric charge can't appear or disappear. This is built directly into Maxwell's equations.

$\rho$ =charge density
$\mathbf{J}$ =current density
conservescharge
Quantum Mechanics

Probability conservation

A particle must be somewhere. Total probability always sums to 1. The Schrödinger equation automatically satisfies this.

$\rho$ =$|\psi|^2$
$\mathbf{J}$ =prob. current
conservesprobability
The big picture: The Schrödinger equation (which we'll cover later) actually implies the continuity equation as a consequence. That's how we know quantum mechanics conserves probability — it's not an assumption, it falls out of the math automatically.

Key takeaways

  • The continuity equation is a statement of conservation — no more, no less.
  • It relates the time rate of change of density to the spatial spreading of flux.
  • It's first order in time: you only need to know the initial density to predict the future (unlike the wave equation, which is second order).
  • The same equation works for fluids, electricity, and quantum mechanics — the physics changes, but the math is identical.
  • In quantum mechanics, it's not an added assumption — it follows automatically from the Schrödinger equation.
